What's the meaning of life?

I don’t think this is a question that comes with a final answer. At least, I have never believed it should. Whenever I hear this question, what interests me is not the solution, but the fact that people keep asking it — again and again, at different stages of their lives.

In the documentary "Examined Life", Cornel West responds to this question:

"I think the problem of meaning is very important. Nihilism is a serious challenge. Meaninglessness is a serious challenge. Even making sense of meaninglessness itself is a kind of discipline and achievement. The problem, of course, is that you never reach it. It's not a static, stationary telos — not an end or final aim. It's a process that one never reaches. It's Sisyphean. You're pushing the rock up the hill looking for better meanings or grander, more ennobling, more enabling meanings, but you never reach it. In that sense you die without having the whole, in the language of romantic discourse."

This idea naturally led me to Albert Camus.

Sisyphus

IN THE MYTH OF SISYPHUS, Camus describes what he calls the “ABSURD”: the tension between our deep desire for meaning and the universe’s complete indifference to that desire. We want answers. The universe offers none. According to Camus, this confrontation is unavoidable.

What struck me most was his insistence on what not to do. Faced with the absurd, Camus rejects both physical suicide and philosophical escape — whether through religion, metaphysics, or blind optimism. To flee from the absurd is to lie to oneself. The only honest response, he argues, is to face it fully and remain conscious within it.

And then there is Sisyphus.

Condemned to push a boulder up a mountain, only to watch it roll back down every time, Sisyphus seems like the ultimate symbol of meaningless labor. Yet Camus famously concludes that “one must imagine Sisyphus happy.” Not because the task changes, but because Sisyphus becomes aware of his condition and chooses to accept it without illusion.

When I think about it, this myth feels uncomfortably familiar. Daily routines, repetitive work, deadlines, commuting — much of modern life resembles pushing the same stone up the same hill. Nothing dramatic happens most of the time. There is no grand revelation waiting at the top. And yet, Camus suggests that meaning does not come from the result, but from the act of pushing itself — from the awareness, persistence, and refusal to surrender consciousness.

This perspective quietly shifts how I think about meaning. Perhaps meaning is not something I am supposed to find. Perhaps it is something that forms as I continue to ask questions, that shows up in my life.

Meaning, then, is not an answer. It is an attitude. A way of staying awake.
