Meaningness

lkhmemories — Dec 16, 2025

What's the meaning of life? (This is not an essay arguing for a definitive answer; it simply shares non-personal perspectives from different angles.)

In the documentary Examined Life, Cornel West responds to this question:

"I think the problem of meaning is very important. Nihilism is a serious challenge. Meaninglessness is a serious challenge. Even making sense of meaninglessness itself is a kind of discipline and achievement. The problem, of course, is that you never reach it. It's not a static, stationary telos — not an end or final aim. It's a process that one never reaches. It's Sisyphean. You're pushing the rock up the hill looking for better meanings or grander, more ennobling, more enabling meanings, but you never reach it. In that sense you die without having the whole, in the language of romantic discourse."

The Myth of Sisyphus (French: Le mythe de Sisyphe) is a philosophical essay Albert Camus published in 1942 that develops a theory of the absurd: the tension between humanity’s fundamental need for meaning and the universe’s indifferent, irrational silence. The central claim is that acknowledging this condition does not justify suicide; instead, it calls for lucid resistance.

The absurd arises from the collision between our “thirst for meaning” and the world’s refusal to answer. This confrontation must be faced directly, without recourse to transcendence, illusion, or metaphysical consolation.

Several possible responses to an absurd life are then examined. In the final chapter, the human condition is compared to the fate of Sisyphus from Greek mythology. Condemned to push a boulder up a mountain only to see it roll back down just before reaching the summit, Sisyphus is trapped in an endless and seemingly meaningless task. Yet the conclusion is paradoxical: “The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man’s heart. One must imagine Sisyphus happy.”

This eternal, futile labor becomes an image of modern existence—of individuals confined to repetitive, mechanical work in factories and offices. Contemporary laborers, who spend their lives performing the same tasks, share an equally absurd fate. Crucially, this condition becomes tragic only once its absurdity is consciously recognized.

The absurdity of life is presented as unavoidable, much like the routines of living, working, studying, and taking examinations. The task, therefore, is not to deny the absurd but to learn how to live with it. Any grasp of meaning remains permanently insufficient; it can never fully mend the fractures of existence. Even when meaning is demanded, it offers no definitive answers. Yet precisely because it is absent, the pursuit continues.

Action persists because human beings continually attempt to compensate for this essential lack. At the core of existence lies a structural deficiency that cannot be resolved. Meaning, then, is not something to be discovered once and for all; it is something to be sought continuously, sustaining the movement of life itself.
