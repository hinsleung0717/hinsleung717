意義 Meaningness｜Soul

lkhmemories — Dec 27, 2025

What's the meaning of life? In a movie called "Soul", it tells:

In Soul, there’s a character named Dez. On the outside, he looks like someone who has it all figured out: a talented barber who clearly enjoys his work. To Joe, the protagonist, it seems obvious that Dez has found his “spark” — the one thing that gives his life meaning. If you are good at something and you love doing it, then that must be your purpose, right?

But when 22, temporarily inhabiting Joe’s body, chats with Dez out of curiosity, a different story emerges. Dez reveals that his real dream was never to become a barber. He wanted to be a veterinarian. Unfortunately, his family could not afford the cost of veterinary school, so he chose a barber school instead, simply because it was cheaper. That practical decision ended up shaping the course of his entire life.

At first, Joe and 22 assume Dez must be living with regret. But Dez surprises them — and me. He says he is genuinely happy. Barbering wasn’t his dream, and he doesn’t believe he was “born” to do it, yet interacting with people, making them feel good about themselves, makes his days meaningful. That subtle revelation felt like a wake-up call: meaning might not come from destiny but from how we show up in ordinary moments.

Nihilism

This idea resonates with Nietzsche’s concept of nihilism, but not the passive kind that simply denies value. Nietzsche described a situation that feels eerily familiar: when the traditional sources of meaning — religion, moral absolutes, social norms — fade, we are left to confront a world without predetermined answers. The challenge is not whether life has meaning in itself, but whether we are willing to live fully and responsibly in a world that offers no guaranteed meaning.

Nietzsche urged what we might call an “active nihilism.” After acknowledging that the world does not hand us meaning on a platter, we are invited to take responsibility for creating value — not once and for all, but continuously, through how we live.

What Soul suggests — quietly and tenderly — is that life’s worth is not found in some grand calling but in the way we engage with the present. A meal shared, a breeze felt, a genuine conversation — these seemingly ordinary moments are where life begins to shine again. Not because they point to a larger destiny, but because they are lived fully.

Nietzsche’s thought experiment of eternal recurrence asks a haunting question: if every moment of your life were to repeat forever, would you choose to live it again, exactly as it is? The film doesn’t give a dramatic or radical answer. Instead, it offers something softer: as long as you are willing to live, to feel, and to connect with the world around you, then even without a “spark” or a grand dream, your life is already worth repeating.

Perhaps a “spark” is not a mission waiting to be discovered, but something that is truly ignited only when we decide to live earnestly.

The meaning of your life is defined the moment you decide to find a meaning for your life.
